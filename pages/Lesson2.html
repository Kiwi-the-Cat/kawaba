Lesson 2 - Basic sentences

To indicate the role that each word plays in a sentence, kawaba uses markers! All grammatical markers take the form {n|'n'} {V|vowel}.

Subject marker {ne|subject marker}

{ne|subject marker} marks the subject of a sentence. In the English sentence "I like birds", "I" is the subject.
It always comes after the word that it is marking.

Open the root tables to try to translate these simple sentences. Remember that in kawaba, any word can take on any part of speech!

{★ |"you are good"} {ga |you} {ne |subject marker} {pe |good}​​ ​{★ |"I eat/drink"} {da | I/me} {ne | subject marker} {min |eat/drink}​ ​​{★ |"a creature dies"} {pun | creature} {ne | subject marker} {ta |die}

Good job! You just read your first kawaba sentences. In kawaba, there is no default number or tense. The last sentence could be translated as {★★★| "creatures are dying"}, {★★★|"a creature died"}, or any other possible combination of numbers and tenses.

Object marker {no|object marker}

{no|object marker} marks the object of a sentence. In the English sentence "I see a cat", "a cat" is the object.
It always comes before the word it is marking.

Try to translate these simple sentences:

{★ |"I see you"} {da |I/me} {ne |subject marker} {wi |see} {no | object marker} {ga | you}​ ​ {★ |"a creature says a word"} {pun | creature} {ne | subject marker} {wa |say} {no | object marker} {ja | word}​ ​ {★ |"You eat/drink this"} {ga |you} {ne | subject marker} {min |eat/drink} {no |object marker} {je | this}

​

Well done! Notice that {ne|subject marker} always comes before the verb, and {no|object marker} always comes directly after.
This makes it easy to tell which word is the verb in a sentence.

Other markers

kawaba has three other markers: {na|context marker}, {ni|location marker}, and {nu|temporal marker}. These are used to add additional information to a sentence.

Context marker {na|context marker}

{na|topic marker} marks the context of a sentence.
In kawaba, the pattern "word na sentence" can be read as:

  • in the context of word, sentence
  • from the viewpoint of word, sentence
  • word; sentence

If you speak toki pona, you can liken na to "la" (but it is not used for time).
Now, try to translate these simple sentences:

{★ |"The truth; I am inside a house."} {ha |true} {na |context marker} {da |I/me} {ne | subject marker} {ho | inside} {no |object marker} {lin|house}​ ​{★ |"From the viewpoint of me, this is good."} {da |I/me} {na |context marker} {je |this} {ne | subject marker} {pe | good}​ ​ {★ | "Maybe; I am a creature"} {tu |maybe} {na |context marker} {da |I/me} {ne | subject marker} {creature|pun}

​

Time and place: {nu|temporal marker} and {ni|location marker}

kawaba uses markers to indicate the contextual factors of time and place.
Try to use your intuition to translate these sentences:

{★ |"In a house, a creature eats."} {lin |house} {ni |location marker} {pun |creature} {ne | subject marker} {min | eats}​ ​{★ |"For a long time, you live" } {lu |long time} {nu |temporal marker} {ga |you} {ne | subject marker} {pa | live}​ ​ {★ | "For a moment in the water, I touch a creature."} {hu |moment} {nu |temporal marker} {gin |water} {ni | location marker} {da | I/me} {ne | subject marker} {ji | touch} {no | object marker} {pun |creature}

{nu|temporal marker}, when used with a non temporal word, can be read as "during" or "while", as in {★|"During sleep, I breathe"} {bun |sleep} {nu |temporal marker} {da | I/me} {ne |subject marker} {kun|breathe}..

Conclusion

Good job on this lesson! We have learned the 5 grammatical markers in kawaba.

  • {ne|subject marker} : topic marker
  • {no|object marker} : object marker
  • {na|context marker} : context marker
  • {nu|temporal marker} : temporal marker
  • {ni|topic marker} : location marker
