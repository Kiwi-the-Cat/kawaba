Lesson 2 - Basic sentences

To indicate the role that a word plays, kawaba uses markers! All grammatical markers take the form nV.

Subject marker {ne|subject marker}

{ne|subject marker} marks the subject of a sentence. In the English sentence "I like birds", "I" is the subject.
It always comes after the word that it is marking.

Open the root tables to try to translate these simple sentences - hover for the answers.
Remember that in kawaba, any word can take on any part of speech!

{★ |"you are good"} {ga |you} {ne |subject marker} {pe |good}​ ​{★ |"a creature dies"} {pun | creature} {ne | subject marker} {ta |die}​ ​{★ |"I eat/drink"} {da | I/me} {ne | subject marker} {min |eat/drink}

Good job! You just read your first kawaba sentences. In kawaba, there is no default number or tense. The last two sentences could be translated as {★★★| "creatures are dying"} and {★★★|"I am eating"} (or any other way you can think of), respectively.

Object marker {no|object marker}

{no|object marker} marks the object of a sentence. In the English sentence "I see a cat", "a cat" is the object.
It always comes before the word it is marking.

Try to translate these simple sentences:

{★ |"I see you"} {da |I/me} {ne |subject marker} {wi |see} {no | object marker} {ga | you}​ ​ {★ |"a creature says a word"} {pun | creature} {ne | subject marker} {wa |say} {no | object marker} {ja | word}​ ​ {★ |"You eat/drink this"} {ga |you} {ne | subject marker} {min |eat/drink} {no |object marker} {je | this}

​

Well done! Notice that {ne|subject marker} always comes before the verb, and {no|object marker} always comes directly after.
This makes it easy to tell which word is the verb in a sentence.

Other markers

kawaba has three other markers: {na|topic marker}, {ni|location marker}, and {nu|context marker}. These are used to add additional information to a sentence.

Topic marker {na|topic marker}

{na|topic marker} marks the topic of a sentence.
It can be compared to the Japanese particle は. In the English sentence "As for dinner, I ate with my father", "dinner" is the topic.

In kawaba, the pattern "word na sentence" can be read as:

- [as] for word, sentence
- word: sentence
- word is such that sentence

Try to translate these simple sentences:

{★ |"True: I am inside a house."} {ha |true} {na |topic marker} {da |I/me} {ne | subject marker} {ho | inside} {no |object marker} {lin|house}​ ​{★ |"For me, this is good."} {da |I/me} {na |topic marker} {je |this} {ne | subject marker} {pe | good}
